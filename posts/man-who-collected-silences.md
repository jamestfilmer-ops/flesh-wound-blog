title: "The Man Who Collected Silences"

excerpt: "A short story about what we keep, and what we call it."

He had started, as most collectors do, without intending to. The first silence he kept was the one after his mother's last word to him, which had been his name, spoken from a hospital bed with something he chose to interpret as approval. He held it in his chest for the drive home. He did not play the radio.

The second was the silence of the flat when he returned from her funeral. Not the heavy silence of grief — he had expected that, had braced for it as one braces for cold water. It was the other kind. The silence of a space that had reorganised itself around an absence and found that it fit better this way.

By the third silence he understood that he was doing something deliberate. He began to keep notes. Not of what the silence followed, but of its quality — the way some silences rang slightly, like a glass that had just been set down, and others were flat and final, like felt.

He told no one about this. He was not embarrassed by it, precisely. He simply did not know how to explain a collection that had no form, no location, and no way of being shown to a visitor.

"What do you collect?" a woman asked him once, at a dinner party, the way people ask such questions — looking for the charming answer, the unusual hobby that would make the rest of the table lean in.

He thought for a moment. "Moments after things," he said. She nodded as if this was a normal answer. Perhaps, he thought, it was.
